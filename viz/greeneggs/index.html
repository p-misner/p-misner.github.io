Green Eggs and Ham

The legend goes that Theodor "Dr. Seuss" Geisel's publisher bet him $100 that he couldn't write a book with less than 50 words. He took his publisher up on the bet and the end result was the children's classic: Green Eggs and Ham. I wanted to explore the patterns and data in the fifty words behind the story of Sam-I-am and his quest to get people to eat both green eggs and ham.

At one appearance, the word 'if' is Green Eggs and Ham's least common word. It is only used in the sentence "If you will let me be, I will try them." The words "I" and "not" tie for most frequently used words at 84 appearances each. The 50 words that appear in the story are as follows:

A key feature of 'Green Eggs and Ham' is the reptitive nature of the sentences that gives the story it's distinctive, lyrical tone. Sentences structures like: "Would you like them here or there? Would you like them in a house? Would you like them with a mouse?" are common. These three sentences have identical starts and differ in their last three words. To get a look at closely related sentences are and how they diverege, the sentences in Green Eggs and Ham can be represented as a branching tree diagram.

Eight sentences in the book start with the word 'Would' and all eight of them are followed by the word 'you'. 'Would you?' appears as a complete sentence three times, while the other sentences starting with 'Would' are all exactly seven words in length. Depicting the sentences as a tree diagram, you can see what sentence beginnings are common and what word combinations are unique.
